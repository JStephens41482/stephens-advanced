The Market

Everyone is fighting over the small triangle.

The fire-protection software industry sells exclusively to mid-market shops with office staff who can absorb back-office work. The bottom of the pyramid — solo and very small operators — is structurally ignored. Not because there's no demand. Because their unit economics break below 5 seats.

  • Top — Mid-market shops
    fireNspec, Brycer, Inspect Point, Building Reports. They charge $50/seat/month and need 5+ seats to justify a sales call. Saturated. Defended.
  • Middle — Small shops
    2–4 techs with one office person. Some use the mid-market tools, most use spreadsheets and QuickBooks. Soft target.
  • Bottom — Solo + 1-tech
    The ignored majority. Doing real work, billing real money, on a clipboard. This is technicians.work.

Why This Works

Four mechanics. Each one is a textbook.

None of these are speculative. Each one has been proven in another industry. The novelty is putting them together for the first time in field service.

01 / Christensen

Low-end disruption

Mid-market incumbents cannot follow downmarket profitably — their cost structure, sales motion, and per-seat pricing all break below 5 seats. We enter the segment they've abandoned. By the time they notice, we've moved up the pyramid.

02 / Freemium

Trust before money

Free tier is genuinely excellent and stays free forever. Building trust + credibility with a working tech is what earns the Riker conversion later. The free app is not a trial — it's the relationship.

03 / Anchoring

Receptionist math

$800/mo looks expensive next to other software. Next to a $800/week part-time receptionist, it's a quarter of the price for someone who works 24/7 and never quits. The anchor sells itself.

04 / Network effects

The marketplace moat

Software can be cloned. A network of 5,000 connected solo techs trading work cannot. The marketplace is what locks the company in — once a tech has a referral history on the platform, leaving means losing the network.

The Conversion Mechanic

The product is the funnel.

Most SaaS spends $5–$50 in customer-acquisition cost per signup on Google and Meta ads. Our marketing budget is zero. Here's why.

The Green Dot

The first time a free user opens the app, we tell them once:

"See this green dot? Every time it lights up, it means you just did something the long way. Riker could've done it for you while you were driving. Riker is a paid add-on — first 30 days free."

From that moment on, every single action a free user takes — every job they create, every invoice they send, every payment they log — pulses the dot for one second. Action by action, the user generates their own upsell pitch. No salesperson. No email funnel. No retargeting ads.

They convert exactly when their accumulated pain crosses the $800/mo math. Some convert in week 2. Some convert in year 2. Either way, they convert because they sold themselves.

Level 4 — The Endgame

The marketplace is what makes this a company.

The field app is a tool. Riker is a service. The marketplace is a network. It can't be cloned. Once it has 5,000 contractors trading work, it's the only place a solo fire-protection tech in North America wants to be.

Why It Locks In

Network = switching cost

A tech with 100 completed trades on the platform has a verified reputation that doesn't transfer anywhere else. Leaving means starting over with strangers. The longer they stay, the more they can't leave.

Why fireNspec Can't Build It

Wrong customer

fireNspec sells to one shop at a time. They have zero incentive to connect their customers to each other — that's just helping competitors share work. We sell to thousands of solo guys who individually benefit from being connected.
